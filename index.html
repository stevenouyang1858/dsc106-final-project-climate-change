What are the Implications of Global Warming?

For millions of years, earth’s temperature has warmed and cooled in a cyclic ways driven by natural wonders. However, something different happened in the past two centuries. As industrial revolution happened around the world, humans began releasing an unprecedented amount of CO2, a type of greenhouse gas, into the atmosphere from factories, cars, and the burning of coal, oil, and gas. The scatter plot below proved this trend.
This scatter plot plots the change in the average of bi-annual atmospheric CO2 mass in 10 trillion kilograms compared to the average CO2 mass between the years 1950 to 1980. From the plot, there is a strong correlation between temperature and atmospheric CO2 mass, meaning that the more greenhouse gas emitted from human activity, the hotter planet Earth gets.

To put the greenhouse gas into perspective, here's a line plot with historical atmospheric CO2 mass from 1950 up to 2015.

If we continue the trend of CO2 emissions into the atmosphere, it is estimated that CO2 levels will peak around the year 2100. This assumes that some climate protections are taken and emissions are stabilized at an intermediate level.

If minimal to no climate actions are taken, the amount of CO2 emitted to the atmosphere will fall within the range of the red and yellow lines.

Not All Regions Warm Equally

While global warming affects the entire planet, its impacts are not uniform. Some regions experience warming faster than others due to various factors including geography, ocean currents, and local climate patterns. Select different countries below to see how their temperature anomalies have changed over time. The Melting Ice Caps: A Visible Consequence of Warming

Rising temperatures don't just mean hotter days—they have profound consequences for our planet's ice. This visualization shows how polar sea ice concentration has changed over time. Sea ice acts as a critical component of Earth's climate system, reflecting sunlight and helping regulate global temperatures. As warming continues, we see a dramatic decline in sea ice coverage. The blue line shows historical data, while the red dashed line shows projections if we continue with moderate emissions. Hover over the chart to see specific values and notice how ice coverage has dropped from over 40% in the 1950s to less than 30% projected by 2100. This melting ice contributes to sea level rise and further accelerates warming through reduced reflectivity—a dangerous feedback loop.

We planned out the entire narrative of the project following the “and, but, therefore” structure, as well as planning out what type of graphs we want to use for each section of the project, and planning out the layout and purpose of each visualization that we are going to use.

We implemented two different working, but not perfect yet, visualizations in D3 on our page so far. For our first visualization, we wanted to show the correlation between the mass of CO2 in our atmosphere and land surface temperature. Using a scatterplot, we plotted the data points and included a hover interaction that shows the year of each data point.
